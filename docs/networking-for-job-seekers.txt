Networking for Job Seekers

Up to 80% of jobs are filled through networking — many positions are never publicly posted. Building and leveraging a professional network is one of the most powerful career development activities you can invest in, and it is a skill that compounds over time.

THE MINDSET SHIFT
Networking is not about asking people for jobs. It is about building genuine professional relationships over time. People help people they know, like, and trust. The goal of most networking interactions is not to get a job — it is to get information, advice, or a warm introduction.

STARTING WITH YOUR EXISTING NETWORK
Your network is larger than you think. Start with:
- Former colleagues and managers
- College classmates and professors
- People you have met at conferences or meetups
- LinkedIn connections you have not spoken to recently
- Friends and family who work in relevant industries

Reach out to reconnect, not to ask for a job. A simple message: "Hey [Name], it has been a while — I saw your post about [topic] and wanted to reconnect. I'm exploring new opportunities in [field] and would love to catch up for 20 minutes."

INFORMATIONAL INTERVIEWS
An informational interview is a 20–30 minute conversation where you ask someone about their career path, company, or industry — not to ask for a job, but to learn. These conversations often lead to job opportunities organically.

How to request one:
"Hi [Name], I came across your profile and was really impressed by your work on [specific thing]. I'm currently exploring a transition into [field] and would love to hear about your experience. Would you have 20 minutes for a virtual coffee in the next few weeks?"

Questions to ask:
- How did you get into this field?
- What does a typical day look like in your role?
- What skills are most valuable in this industry right now?
- What advice would you give someone trying to break in?
- Is there anyone else you would recommend I speak with?

Always send a thank-you note afterward and follow up on any referrals they offered.

EXPANDING YOUR NETWORK
- Attend industry meetups, conferences, and professional association events
- Join relevant LinkedIn groups and participate in discussions
- Contribute to open-source projects or online communities in your field
- Speak at or volunteer at industry events
- Follow and engage with thought leaders in your space

WARM INTRODUCTIONS
A warm introduction — where someone in your network introduces you to a hiring manager or key person — is worth ten cold applications. When asking for an introduction, make it easy for the connector: provide a brief summary of why you are reaching out and draft a short introduction they can forward.

STAYING IN TOUCH
The biggest networking mistake is only reaching out when you need something. Maintain relationships by:
- Sharing relevant articles or resources with your contacts
- Congratulating people on promotions or new roles
- Commenting thoughtfully on their LinkedIn posts
- Sending a quick check-in message once or twice a year

FOLLOWING UP AFTER APPLYING
If you have a connection at a company you applied to, reach out: "Hey [Name], I just applied for the [role] at [Company] and wanted to let you know — I'm really excited about the opportunity. If you have any insights about the team or role, I'd love to hear them."

A well-timed message to an internal contact can move your resume from the bottom of the pile to the top.
