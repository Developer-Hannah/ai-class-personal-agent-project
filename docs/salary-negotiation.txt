Salary Negotiation Strategies

Negotiating your salary is one of the highest-return activities in your career. A successful negotiation at a new job can be worth tens of thousands of dollars in lifetime earnings, since future raises and bonuses are often calculated as a percentage of base salary.

THE CORE PRINCIPLE
Negotiation is expected. Hiring managers anticipate it. Making a counter-offer does not make you look greedy — it signals that you know your value and can advocate for yourself. Most initial offers have room built in.

DO YOUR RESEARCH FIRST
Know your market value before any salary conversation. Sources:
- Glassdoor, Levels.fyi (for tech), LinkedIn Salary, Bureau of Labor Statistics
- Talk to recruiters at other companies — they will tell you what the market pays
- Ask peers in similar roles what compensation ranges look like

Target a number at the 75th percentile for your role, experience level, and location. Know your true floor — the minimum you would accept — but never lead with it.

WHEN THE OFFER COMES
Do not accept on the spot. Say: "Thank you so much — I'm very excited about this opportunity. Can I have a few days to review the full package?" This is completely normal and expected.

Review the full compensation package:
- Base salary
- Equity (options or RSUs) — vesting schedule, cliff, strike price
- Signing bonus
- Annual bonus target and structure
- Benefits (health, dental, 401k match, PTO)
- Remote work policy and flexibility

MAKING THE COUNTER-OFFER
Counter in writing or on a call — both are fine. Be specific: give a number, not a range. A range signals the bottom of the range is acceptable.

Script: "I've done some research on the market and given my experience in [specific area], I was expecting something closer to $X. Is there flexibility to get there?"

A common mistake is apologizing or using hedging language ("I don't know if this is reasonable, but..."). Be confident and direct.

NEGOTIATING BEYOND BASE SALARY
If the company cannot move on base salary, ask about:
- Signing bonus (often easier to approve, comes from a different budget)
- Additional equity
- Earlier performance review (e.g., 6 months instead of 12)
- Extra PTO days
- Remote work flexibility
- Professional development budget

THE COMPETING OFFER TACTIC
If you have another offer, you can use it as leverage: "I have another offer at $X. I would prefer to join your company, but I need to get closer to that number to make the decision easy." Only use a competing offer if it is real — lying destroys trust.

WHEN THEY SAY NO
If the final answer is still lower than you wanted, decide: is this still the right opportunity? If yes, ask what it would take to reach your target over time: "I understand. Can we agree to revisit my compensation at my 6-month review, with a specific target?"

INTERNAL RAISES AND PROMOTIONS
Ask for raises proactively — do not wait for a manager to offer. Build the case: document your impact, research market rates, and schedule a dedicated meeting (not at the end of a 1:1). Timing matters: after a big win or during annual review cycles is ideal.
